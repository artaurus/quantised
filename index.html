    Overview

    Quantum mechanics is the cornerstone of modern physics. In simple words, it illuminates the realm of the very tiny. Despite successfully infiltrating almost every branch of physics, there is no established interpretation of its fundamental principles. Perhaps because it renders an uninvited departure from ordinary human intuition.

    This series of articles could presumably serve as chapters of a book, in which I will elucidate the foundations of the theory while curbing the abstract mathematics. Or at the very least, attempt to do so.

    Introduction
    Max Planck's and Albert Einstein's quantisation of light, Niels Bohr's stationary states, and Werner Heisenberg's matrix mechanics.

    Wave-Particle Duality
    Louis de Broglie's postulated matter waves, Erwin Schrödinger's wavefunction, and Max Born's probabilistic interpretation.

    Transformation Theory
    Concepts behind Paul Dirac's mathematical reconciliation of Heisenberg's matrices and Schrödinger's waves.

    Uncertainty
    Heisenberg's principle visualised as a consequence of wave-particle duality.

    Spin
    Something strange.

    Antiparticles
    A relativistic emergence.

    Entanglement
    David Bohm's nonlocality and John Bell's inequalities.

    Interpretations
    Bohr's Copenhagen, Einstein's realist, and that of many worlds.

    A recommendation is the book 'Quantum' by Manjit Kumar; a captivating exposition, including an extensive historical recollection, of the quantum revolution. For the mathematically inclined, my references are Ramamurti Shankar's 'Principles of Quantum Mechanics', Steven Weinberg's 'Lectures on Quantum Mechanics', and Paul Dirac's 'Lectures on Quantum Mechanics and Relativistic Field Theory'.
